(iii).  Indexing Types -- B-Tree Index
 B-Tree is a hierarchical levels of “index records”
 The lowest level index records point the rows in the data tables
  If a column has many unique vales, then the selectivity of the column is said to high.
 In a “sales territory” dimension table, the “city” column has high selectivity and should be used for Indexing.
  For some columns, the selectivity may not be there.  You can make the selectivity of columns higher by concatenating the columns --- but this increases the size of the “index” file

(iii).  Indexing Type – Bitmapped Index
 Bitmapped is ideally suited for low-selectivity data.
 A bitmap is an ordered series of bits, one for each distinct value of the indexed column.
  Example:
 Product Color – 	white      100
  		almond    010
 			black       001
  Product             – 	dishwasher   001
  		washer    100
 			dryer       010

 Region	  --	east   1000,     west  0100
		north 0010,     south 0001

bit mapped indexes
When a column is said to be highly selective, this means that a very low percentage of the total rows possible is returned which is another way of saying that the column contains a lot of unique values. Columns with high selectivity are often the best candidates for indexes. 
Columns with low selectivity, very few unique values, are often not good candidates for indexes as often the index will be ignored in favor of a table scan or clustered index scan as the index does not offer any benefits to the optimizer.

(vi). Indexing the Dimension Table
  Columns of the dimensional table are used for the predicates of queries
  Create a unique B-Tree index on single-column primary key
  Examine the columns that are commonly used to constrain queries.  These are actually ready for bitmapped indexing.
  Look for columns that are frequently accessed together in large dimension tables – think about multicolumn indexes
